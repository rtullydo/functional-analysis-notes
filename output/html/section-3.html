Section 1.3 The spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators

Our first diagonalization result is a special case, but one that sheds light on the (quite a bit) more complicated general case. This is a good case to get to grips with the point of the theorem because it is so similar to the finite dimensional case (compare with the statement in (1.1.1).

Theorem 1.3.1.

Let \(T\) be a compact self-adjoint operator on a Hilbert space \(\hilbert\text{.}\) Then \(T\) has only countable distinct eigenvalues. Enumerating the eigenvalues as \(\{\la_1, \la_2, \ldots\}\text{,}\) let \(P_n\) denote the projection of \(\hilbert\) onto \(\ker(T - \la_n)\text{.}\) Then \(P_nP_m = P_m P_n = 0\) when \(n \neq m\text{,}\) each \(\la_n\) is real, and

\begin{equation*} T = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \la_n P_n, \end{equation*}

where the sum converges in the norm of \(\BH\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.3.1 Proof of the spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators

Proposition 1.3.2.

If \(A\) is a normal operator and \(\la \in \mathbb{F}\text{,}\) then \(\ker(A - \la) = \ker(A - \la)\ad\) and \(\ker(A - \la)\) is a reducing subspace for \(A\text{.}\)

Proof.

Since \(A\) is normal, it is easy to see that so must be \(A - \la\text{.}\) By [provisional cross-reference: prop-normaladnorm], we have that \(\norm{(A - \la)h} = \norm{(A - \la)\ad h}\) for all \(h\text{.}\) But then \(h \in \ker(A - \ad)\) if and only if \(h \in \ker(A - \la)\ad\text{.}\)

If \(h \in \ker(A - \la)\text{,}\) then \(Ah = \la h \in \ker(A - \la)\) and \(A\ad h = \cc\la h \in \ker(A - \la)\text{.}\) We conclude that \(\ker(A - \la)\) is a reducing subspace for \(A\text{.}\)

The next result is similar to the finite dimensional fact that eigenspaces of distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.

Proposition 1.3.3.

If \(A\) is a normal operator and \(\la, \mu\) are distinct eigenvalues of \(A\) then \(\ker(A - \la)\perp \ker(A - \mu)\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(h \in \ker(A - \la)\) and \(g \in \ker(A - \mu)\text{.}\) By Proposition 1.3.2, we have that \(A\ad g = \cc\mu g\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} \la\ip{h}{g} \amp=\ip{A h}{g}\\ \amp= \ip{h}{A\ad g}\\ \amp= \ip{h}{\cc\mu g}\\ \amp= \mu\ip{h}{g}. \end{align*}

Thus, \((\la - \mu)\ip{h}{g} = 0\text{.}\) Since \(\la, \mu\) are distinct, we must have \(h\perp g\) and thus that \(\ker(A - \la) \perp \ker(A - \mu)\text{.}\)

As with hermitian matrices, the eigenvalues of self-adjoint operators must be real.

Proposition 1.3.4.

If \(A = A\ad\) and \(\la \in \sigma_p(A)\) then \(\la\) is a real number.

Proof.

If \(A h = \la h\) then \(A h = A \ad h = \cc{\la}h\) by Proposition 1.3.2. Equating the two expression for \(Ah\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} (\la - \cc\la)h = 0. \end{equation*}

Choosing a non-zero \(h\) requires that \(\la - \cc\la = 0\text{,}\) or \(\la = \cc\la\text{.}\)

The final ingredient we need is to demonstrate that the spectrum of a compact self-adjoint operator contains non-zero elements. In the case of a finite dimensional diagonalizable matrix, we have that the norm of the map induced by the matrix is equal to the magnitude of the largest eigenvalue. The same result holds in the compact self-adjoint case.

Lemma 1.3.5.

If \(T\) is a compact self-adjoint operator, then \(\norm{T}\) or \(-\norm{T}\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\)

Proof.

If \(T\) is the zero operator, then we're done. If \(T \neq 0\text{,}\) there exists a sequence of unit vectors \(h_n\) so that

\begin{equation*} \abs{\ip{T h_n}{h_n}} \to \norm{T}. \end{equation*}

A limit point argument produces a subsequence \(h_n\) so that

\begin{equation*} \ip{T h_n}{h_n} \to \la \end{equation*}

where \(\abs{\la} = \norm{T}\text{.}\)

Since \(\abs{\la} = \norm{T}\text{,}\) we get

\begin{align*} 0 \amp \leq \norm{(T - \la)h_n}^2\\ \amp= \norm{Th}^2 - 2\la\ip{Th_n}{h_n} + \la^2\\ \amp= 2\la^2 - 2 \la\ip{Th_n}{h_n}. \end{align*}

But \(\ip{Th_n}{h_n} \to \la\text{,}\) so the computation above implies that \(\norm{(T - \la)h_n} \to 0\text{.}\) Applying Proposition 1.2.12, we conclude that \(\la \in \sigma_p(T)\text{.}\)

We are now prepared to prove the spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators. Essentially, we're going to use Lemma 1.3.5 to find an eigenvalue, construct a projection onto its eigenspace, and then restrict the operator to the remaining orthogonal complement. Proceeding inductively will give the result.

Proof of Theorem 1.3.1..

Suppose that \(T\) is a compact self-adjoint operator on a Hilbert space \(\hilbert\text{.}\)

By Proposition 1.3.4 and Lemma 1.3.5, there exists a real number \(\la_1\) in \(\sigma_p(T)\) with \(\abs{\la_1} = \norm{T}\text{.}\) Let \(E_1 = \ker(T - \la_1)\) and \(P_1:\hilbert \to E_1\) be the projection onto \(E_1\text{.}\) Denote by \(\hilbert_2\) the orthogonal complement of \(E_1\) in \(\hilbert\text{.}\) By Proposition 1.3.2, \(E_1\) reduces \(T\text{,}\) and so \(\hilbert_2\) reduces \(T\text{.}\) Then define \(T_2\) to be the restriction of \(T\) to \(\hilbert_2\text{;}\) that is \(T_2 = T_{\hilbert_2}\text{.}\)
