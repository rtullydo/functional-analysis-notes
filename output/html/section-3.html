Section 1.3 Compact operators

The object of study in this section is essentially the most direct generalization of matrices to infinite dimensions, for reasons that will become apparent.

Definition 1.3.1.

Suppose \(\hilbert\) is a Hilbert space. A linear map \(T: \hilbert \to \hilbert\) is compact if the image of the unit ball in \(\hilbert\) under \(T\text{,}\) denoted \(T(\ball \hilbert)\text{,}\) has compact closure in \(\hilbert\text{.}\)

Denote by \(\BOP_0(\hilbert, \K)\) the set of compact operators from \(\hilbert\) to \(\K\text{,}\) and \(\BOP_0(\hilbert) = \BOP_0(\hilbert, \hilbert)\text{.}\)

Proposition 1.3.2.

\(\BOP_0(\hilbert,\K) \subset \BOP(\hilbert, \K)\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(T \in \BOP_0(\hilbert, \K)\text{.}\) Since \(T(\ball \hilbert)\) is compact, there must exist a constant \(C\) so that \(T(\ball \hilbert) \subset B_\K(C) = \{k \in \K : \norm{k} \leq C\}\text{.}\) But then \(\norm{T} \leq C\text{,}\) and so \(T \in \BH\text{.}\)

Proposition 1.3.3.

\(\BOP_0(\hilbert, \K)\) is a linear space.

Proposition 1.3.4.

If \(T_n\) is a sequence in \(\BOP_0(\hilbert, \K)\) and \(T\) is an operator in \(\BOP(\hilbert, \K)\) such that \(\norm{T_n - T} \to 0\text{,}\) then \(T\) is compact.

Proposition 1.3.5.

If \(A \in \BOP(\hilbert)\text{,}\) \(B \in \BOP(\K)\text{,}\) and \(T \in \BOP_0(\hilbert, \K)\text{,}\) then \(TA\) and \(BT\) are compact.

A special family of compact operators is even more restricted by the size of their ranges. Say that an operator is of finite rank, if \(\ran T\) is finite dimensional (that is, the closed linear span of a finite set of vectors). Let \(\BOP_{00}(\hilbert, \K)\) be the set of continuous finite rank operators. It is straightfoward that \(\BOP_{00}\) is a linear space and that \(\BOP_{00} \subset \BOP_0\text{.}\) It turns out to be the case that compact operators are (norm) limits of operators of finite rank.

Theorem 1.3.6.

Let \(T \in \BOP(\hilbert, \K)\text{.}\) The following are equivalent.

  1. \(T\) is compact.

  2. \(T\ad\) is compact.

  3. There is a sequence \(T_n\) of operators of finite rank such that \(\norm{T_n - T} \to 0\text{.}\)

Proof.

First, note that \((3) \Rightarrow (1)\) follows from Proposition 1.3.4.

We will show that \((1) \Rightarrow (3) \Rightarrow (2) \Rightarrow (1)\text{.}\)

\((1) \Rightarrow (3)\text{:}\) We use the fact that since \(T\) is compact, the closure of \(T(\ball \hilbert)\) contains a countable dense subset (one can construct this set using the fact that the image of the ball under \(T\) is totally bounded). Then \(\mathcal L = \cl \ran T\) is a separable subspace of \(\K\text{.}\) Let \(\{e_j\}\) be a basis for \(\mathcal L\text{,}\) and let \(P_n\) be the projection of \(\K\) onto the closed linear span of the first \(n\) basis vectors; that is, \(P_n\) is the projection onto \(\vee\{e_j: 1 \leq j \leq n\}\text{.}\) Now set \(T_n = P_n T\text{.}\) Each \(T_n\) is a finite rank operator.

We will show that \(\norm{T_n - T} \to 0\) by establishing a uniform bound on \(\norm{T_n h - T h}\) for \(\norm{h}\leq 1\text{.}\) Choose \(\eps > 0\text{.}\) By compactness, we can find a finite collection of vectors \(h_1, \ldots, h_m\) so that

\begin{equation*} T(\ball \hilbert) \subset \bigcup_{j = 1}^m B(Th_j, \ep/3). \end{equation*}

For \(h \in \ball \hilbert\text{,}\) choose \(h_j\) so that \(\norm{Th - Th_j} \leq \eps/3\text{.}\) Then for any \(n \in \N\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} \norm{T_n h - T h} \amp\leq \norm{T_n h - T_n h_j } + \norm{T_n h_j - T h_j} + \norm{T h_j - T h} \\ \amp \leq\norm{P_n(T h - T h_j) } + \norm{T_n h_j - T h_j} + \norm{T h_j - T h} \\ \amp\leq 2\norm{T h_j - T h} + \norm{T_n h_j - T h_j}\\ \amp\leq \frac{2\eps}{3} + \norm{T_n h_j - T h_j}. \end{align*}

Now, we show that we can find \(n_0\) so that \(\norm{T_n h_j - T h_j} \leq \eps/3\text{.}\) Certainly \(Th_j \in \mathcal{L}\text{,}\) and so can be expanded as \(Th_j = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ip{Th_j}{ e_k} e_k\text{.}\) On the other hand,

\begin{equation*} T_n h_j = P_n T h_j = \sum_{k=1}^n \ip{Th_j}{ e_k} e_k \end{equation*}

Together, this gives \(\norm{T_n h_j - T h_j} \to 0\text{,}\) and so the desired \(n_0\) exists. Since \(h\) was an arbitrary vector with \(\norm{h} \leq 1\text{,}\) this bound is uniform, and so in the operator norm we have \(\norm{T_n - T} \to 0\text{.}\)

\(3 \Rightarrow 2:\) Suppose that \(T_n\) is a sequence in \(\BOP_{00}(\hilbert, \K)\) with \(\norm{T_n - T} \to 0\text{.}\) Then \(\norm{T_n\ad - T\ad} = \norm{T_n - T} \to 0\text{.}\) But \(T_n\ad\) is also in \(\BOP_{00}(\hilbert, \K)\) (Exercise). Since \(3 \Rightarrow 1\text{,}\) \(T\ad\) is compact.

\(2 \Rightarrow 1\text{:}\) (Exercise)

We used an important convergence theorem about finite rank projections in the proof above, so we record the following corollary of the proof.

Proposition 1.3.7.

If \(T \in \BOP_0(\hilbert, \K)\text{,}\) then \(\cl \ran T\) is separable. If \(\{e_j\}\) is a basis for \(\cl \ran T\) and \(P_n\) is the projection of \(\K\) onto \(\vee\{e_j:1 \leq j\leq n\}\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \norm{P_n T - T} \to 0. \end{equation*}

This allows a method for constructing compact operators, at least in separable Hilbert spaces, in a manner suggestive of finite dimensional eigenvalues.

Proposition 1.3.8.

Let \(\hilbert\) be a separable Hilbert space with basis \(\{e_j\}\text{.}\) Let \(\{\alpha_n\}\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{F}\) with \(M = \sup_n\{\abs{\alpha_n}\} \lt \infty\text{.}\) Define an operator \(A\) on the basis \(\{e_j\}\)by \(A e_j = \alpha_j e_j\) for all \(j\text{.}\) Then \(A\) extends by linearity to a bounded operator on \(\hilbert\) with \(\norm{A} = M\text{.}\) The operator \(A\) is compact if and only if \(\alpha_n \to 0\text{.}\)

We are now ready to make explicit the apparent connection to eigenvalues of matrices acting on finite dimensional spaces.

Definition 1.3.9.

Suppose that \(A \in \BH\text{.}\) A scalar \(\alpha \in \mathbb{F}\) is called an eigenvalue of \(A\) if \(\ker(A - \alpha) \neq \{0\}\text{.}\) A nonzero vector \(h\) in \(\ker(A - \alpha)\) is called an eigenvector of \(A\text{.}\) We recover the usual relationship \(Ah = \alpha h\) for such a pair. Let \(\sigma_p(A)\) denote the set of eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)

The \(p\) in the notation \(\sigma_p(A)\) refers to the point spectrum of more general operators, which we'll talk about later.

Unlike linear operators on finite dimensional spaces, compact operators need not possesses eigenvalues. The prototypical example is the Volterra operator.

Proposition 1.3.10.

Let \(\hilbert = L^2[0,1]\text{.}\) For \(t \in [0,1]\text{,}\) let \(V:L^2 \to L^2\) be the operator

\begin{equation*} V(f)(x) = \int_0^x f(y) \, dy. \end{equation*}

The operator \(V\) is compact. However, \(V\) has no eigenvalues.

The whole thrust of our conversation is diagonalization of operators in terms of eigen-like structures, and here we've identified a compact operator that cannot be. It's worth asking why. Note that \(V\ad(f)(x) = \int_x^1 f(y) \, dy\text{;}\) that is, \(V\) isn't self-adjoint (in fact, \(V\) is also not normal). Keep that in mind as we proceed.

If, however, a compact operator does have an eigenvalue, nice things happen.

Proposition 1.3.11.

If \(T \in \BOP_0(\hilbert)\) and \(\la \in \sigma_p(T)\) with \(\la \neq 0\text{,}\) then the eigenspace \(\ker(T - \la)\) is finite-dimensional.

Proof.

Proceed by contradiction. Suppose that \(\ker(T - \alpha)\) is infinite-dimensional and so contains an infinite orthonormal sequence. Because \(T\) is compact, \(Te_n\) has a convergent subsequence \(T_{e_{n_k}}\text{.}\) Then as a sequence, \(\{T e_{n_k}\}\) is Cauchy. But for any \(n_k \neq n_j\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \norm{T e_{n_k} - T e_{n_j}}^2 = \norm{\la e_{n_k} - \la e_{n_j}}^2 = 2\abs{\la}^2 > 0. \end{equation*}

This contradicts the Cauchyness of \(\{T e_{n_k}\}\text{.}\) We conclude that \(\ker(T - \alpha)\) is finite-dimensional.

Proposition 1.3.12.

If \(T \in \BOP_0(\hilbert)\text{,}\) \(\la \neq 0\text{,}\) and \(\inf\{\norm{(T - \la)h}: \norm{h}=1\}=0\) then \(\la \in \sigma_p(T)\text{.}\)

Proof.

The hypotheses imply that there exists a sequence of unit vectors \(h_n\) so that \(\norm{(T - \la)h_n} \to 0\text{.}\) By compactness, this sequence has a subsequence \(h_{n_k}\) and a vector \(f\) so that \(\norm{Th_{n_k} - f}\to 0\text{.}\) Combining these two facts, we can use an algebra trick to get

\begin{align*} h_{n_k} = \la\inv \la h_{n_k} \amp= \la\inv \la(h_{n_k} - \la\inv T h_{n_k} + \la \inv T h_{n_k})\\ \amp=\la\inv [(\la - T) h_{n_k}) + T h_{n_k}] \\ \amp\to \la\inv[0 + f] = \la\inv f. \end{align*}

Since the \(h_n\) are unit vectors, so is any limit point of the sequence, and so \(1 = \norm{\la\inv f} = \abs{\la}\inv \norm{f}\text{,}\) and so \(\norm{f} \neq 0\text{.}\) Furthermore, \(T h_{n_k} \to \la\inv Tf\text{.}\) Since we also have \(T h_{n_k} \to f\text{,}\) it must be that \(f = \la\inv T f\text{,}\) or rather that \(T f = \la f\text{.}\) That is, \(f\neq 0\text{,}\) \(f \in \ker(T - \la)\text{,}\) which gives that \(\la \in \sigma_p(T)\text{.}\)
